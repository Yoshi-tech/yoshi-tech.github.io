1B: WHAT I WISH I KNEW GOING IN
First term is done. Looking back at the chaos of 1A — what I'd tell myself on day one.
▾

1A hit differently than I expected. I came in thinking it would be an extension of high school CS — write some code, pass some tests, move on. It's not that. University CS expects you to think, not just execute. That distinction took me a few weeks to actually internalize.

THE THING ABOUT PROOFS

MATH 135 — Algebra for Honours Mathematics — was the biggest shift. I'd never written a real proof before. The first few weeks felt like learning a new language. Eventually something clicks: a proof isn't about computation, it's about constructing an airtight argument. Once that landed, I started enjoying it.

ON BEING THE ONLY PROGRAMMER IN THE ROOM

For the SE 101 team project, I ended up doing most of the actual coding. That's fine — I like coding. But I learned that writing software other people need to understand is a completely different skill than writing software that just works. Documentation isn't optional.

WHAT I'D TELL 1A-ME

Start assignments the day they're released, not the day before they're due. Go to office hours even when you think you understand the material. And sleep — seriously, the all-nighter math does not add up.

FEBRUARY 2026 TECH
BUILDING A 3D RENDERER FROM SCRATCH: WHAT I LEARNED
No libraries. No tutorials for the hard parts. Just math, C++, and a lot of wrong triangles on screen.
▾

PRISM started as a question: how hard could it be to draw a triangle on screen without OpenGL? The answer is: harder than you think, but not impossible — and every step of figuring it out teaches you something real.

THE MATH FIRST

Before any pixels, I built the math library from scratch. Vector and matrix operations, dot products, cross products, perspective projection. No external dependencies. Getting the projection matrix right took embarrassingly long — I kept confusing row-major and column-major conventions.

WIN32 AND THE FRAME BUFFER

I used Win32 GDI to push pixels to screen. The trick is a memory-mapped buffer — you write raw pixel data to memory and blit it to the window. Once that pipeline worked, everything else felt tractable. First triangle on screen was a genuinely exciting moment.

WHAT THE PROJECT ACTUALLY TAUGHT ME

Mostly: respect for abstraction layers. Every library you use is hiding enormous complexity. Building from scratch gives you a mental model of what's really happening — and that model pays dividends when something breaks in a higher-level project.

JANUARY 2026 CS CONCEPTS
POINTER ARITHMETIC IS ACTUALLY BEAUTIFUL
Everyone says pointers are confusing. I used to agree. Then something clicked.
▾

Pointers have a reputation. Ask any intro CS student and they'll tell you pointers are the thing that broke their brain. I was the same — until I started working with memory-mapped buffers in PRISM and the model finally snapped into place.

THE MENTAL MODEL THAT HELPED

Stop thinking of a pointer as a "reference to a variable." Think of it as a number — specifically, an address in a giant flat array called RAM. A int* is just a number that says "look at index N in memory and interpret those 4 bytes as an integer." Pointer arithmetic is just index arithmetic on that array.

WHERE IT GETS ELEGANT

Once you see pointers as addresses, things like *(ptr + 3) become obvious rather than cryptic. You're just offsetting your array index. Iterating over a buffer with a pointer is cleaner and often faster than indexed access. And suddenly, the linked list makes total visual sense in memory.

I'm not going to pretend dangling pointers and memory leaks aren't annoying. They are. But the underlying model is genuinely beautiful once it clicks.

NOVEMBER 2025 LIFE
48 HOURS: BUILDING A GAME FOR NASA SPACE APPS
Sole programmer. 5-person team. A complete FPS game in two days. I didn't sleep. I didn't want it to end.
▾

NASA Space Apps is a global hackathon. You get a NASA-provided challenge, 48 hours, and a team. Our challenge involved making a concept for public science education. We decided the concept was: a game.

THE PITCH

Your ship crash-lands on an alien planet. The ecosystem runs on chemosynthesis — life without sunlight. You have to explore, gather data, and figure out how life works here. First-person, full physics, built in Unity.

BEING THE ONLY PROGRAMMER

Five people on the team. Four of them had ideas. One of them (me) had to turn those ideas into code in 48 hours. The scope kept growing — everyone had one more thing they wanted. I learned to say no, then yes to the important stuff, then stay up until 4am making it work.

WHAT SHIPPED

A functional FPS with player movement, a physics system, an exploration loop, a UI, and environmental storytelling. All in C# and Unity, from scratch, in two days. It wasn't perfect. It was real. That mattered.
